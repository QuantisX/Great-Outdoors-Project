Washington State Permits & Passes

Permits

Some wilderness trails, state parks, and camps require permits or passes to help manage visitor numbers and protect natural resources. Requirements vary by location and duration of visit. Some areas require advanced reservations or operate on a first-come, first-serve basis (e.g., Mount Rainier National Park, Olympic National Park).

Discover Pass

The Discover Pass grants access to state-managed recreation lands in Washington, including state parks, wildlife areas, and recreation lands.

  • Annual Discover Pass: Provides unlimited access for a year.
  • One-Day Discover Pass: Grants access for a single day.

Passes can be purchased online, at retail locations, or kiosks in some parks.

Northwest Forest Pass

A Northwest Forest Pass is required for parking at specific recreation sites in national forests across Washington and Oregon.

Backcountry Passes

  • Sno-Park Permits: Required for winter activities like cross-country skiing, snowmobiling, and snowshoeing (November to March).
  • Backcountry Ski Passes: Required for access to certain ski areas offering off-piste or uphill skiing.
  • Wilderness Area Permits: Needed for overnight stays or remote wilderness areas, issued by the U.S. Forest Service or National Park Service.

Each pass supports trail maintenance, education programs, and environmental conservation efforts.

Discover Pass 
 Discover Pass | Washington State Parks
A Washington State Discover Pass is a permit that grants access to state-managed recreation lands in Washington. It allows individuals to park at and use a variety of state parks, wildlife areas, and recreation lands. These lands are managed by the Washington State Parks and Recreation Commission, the Department of Fish and Wildlife, and the Department of Natural Resources.

The Discover Pass is required for most state recreation areas, including parks, trailheads, and some boat launches, and it helps support the maintenance and operation of these areas. There are a couple of options available:

  • Annual Discover Pass: Provides unlimited access to state parks and other recreation lands for a full year from the purchase date.
  • One-Day Discover Pass: Allows access for a single day.

The pass can be purchased online, at participating retail locations, or through a kiosk in some parks. Some locations also offer a two-day pass , and a portion of the revenue supports park and land maintenance. It's a great way to support the preservation of Washington's natural resources and enjoy the outdoors!

A Northwest Forest Pass is a recreational pass that provides access to national forests and recreation areas in the Pacific Northwest of the United States. This pass is typically required for visitors who plan to park at specific recreation sites, like trailheads, campgrounds, or day-use areas in national forests.

It covers areas in states such as Washington and Oregon and is used to support the upkeep of these recreation areas. The pass is available for purchase as an annual or daily pass, depending on the needs of the visitor.

National Forest Recreation ePass (National Forests in Washington and Oregon only): DiscoverNW.org

A backcountry pass refers to a permit or access pass required to use certain backcountry areas for outdoor recreation, such as hiking, skiing, snowboarding, or snowshoeing. These passes are often needed in areas that are not served by traditional ski lifts or other infrastructure but are popular for wilderness experiences, including National Forests, wilderness areas, and certain ski areas.

There are different types of backcountry passes, including:

  1. Sno-Park Permits: These are needed for winter recreation in designated Sno-Park areas. They are required for activities like cross-country skiing, snowmobiling, and snowshoeing. These permits are typically issued by the Washington State Parks and can be used in the winter months, from November to March.
  2. Backcountry Ski Passes: Some backcountry areas, especially ski areas that offer off-piste or uphill skiing access, may require a specific pass or permit. For example, certain resorts or backcountry zones might require you to buy a pass to access the area.
  3. Wilderness Area Permits: These are required for accessing specific wilderness areas, especially if you're planning an overnight stay or heading into more remote parts of the state. Permits are generally available through the U.S. Forest Service or the National Park Service.

Each type of pass typically helps fund trail maintenance, education programs, and environmental protection efforts in the backcountry. It's important to check the specific area you plan to visit for any required passes or permits.
